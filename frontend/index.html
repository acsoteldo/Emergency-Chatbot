About the Project

A Model Built on Real Crisis Data

This project builds a linear programming optimization model to answer a deceptively simple question: given the real humanitarian budget deployed in the Caribbean and Central America between 2019 and 2024, how should it have been allocated based on where need was greatest?

Need is measured by displacement, climate event severity, institutional risk, and coping capacity sourced from UNHCR, EM-DAT, NOAA, INFORM, and OCHA FTS. The gap between actual and optimal funding reveals a systemic pattern of misallocation driven by donor priorities, geopolitical visibility, and migration management rather than humanitarian need.

Alongside the analysis, Ávila translates these findings into actionable guidance for people on the ground, connecting refugees and displaced persons with the organizations active in their country.

Key Findings

Three Things the Numbers Reveal

01
Underfunding

Haiti needed $581M more. It did not get it.

Despite the highest composite need score in the region across all six years, Haiti received $1.02B against a model-optimal $1.60B. The gap of $581M represents 21% of the entire regional budget left undeployed where it was most needed.

02
Misalignment

Migration corridors captured funding meant for crisis zones.

Honduras, Panama, and Costa Rica together received $575M more than their need scores justify. Donor priorities around migration management systematically displaced acute humanitarian investment.

03
Invisibility

Puerto Rico received $0 from international humanitarian systems.

As a US territory, Puerto Rico is excluded from UNHCR and OCHA coverage. The model estimates $32.6M in unmet need over six years. US territorial status creates a structural blind spot in global humanitarian architecture.

Known Limitations

What This Model Does Not Claim

01
UNHCR Displacement is an Annual Proxy

Monthly displacement figures for BHS, BLZ, BRB, CUB, HTI, JAM, NIC, PRI, SLV, and SUR are derived from UNHCR annual stock totals divided by 12, producing a flat monthly baseline with no within-year variation. Disaster shocks from EM-DAT and NOAA partially compensate in event months.

02
INFORM Scores Run Only Through 2021

INFORM risk indices are forward-filled from 2021 through 2024 using the last available annual value. Vulnerability and coping capacity do not reflect post-2021 structural changes, a meaningful gap given Haiti's political deterioration and post-Dorian recovery in the Bahamas.

03
Panama: Transit Displacement Excluded

Panama's primary displacement dynamic is the Darien Gap migration corridor, a transit population largely absent from UNHCR in-country statistics. The model uses UNHCR inflows only, likely underestimating Panama's operational burden.

04
EM-DAT Missing Month Fields

Several EM-DAT disaster records lack a specific start month. These events are assigned to June by convention, which may misplace disaster shocks within a given year. The annual disaster signal is preserved but monthly precision is reduced.

05
Mexico Excluded by Design

Mexico is a transit and receiving country at the end of the Central America corridor, not a displacement origin. Its INFORM risk scores are significantly lower than Tier-1 countries and FTS flows skew toward development and migration management. The corridor pressure Mexico experiences is captured upstream through Colombia and Venezuela as pressure inputs.
